**1600/1700**:
- Children viewed as smaller adults
	- Smaller workers, farmhands, etc.
- Most "literature" is just oral folk tales
- John Locke (the fuck) believed that children should be treated more gently than adults and books should be written for them
	- Children are a blank slate, and should be handled more carefully
	- Took a while to take hold (~200 years)
- Most literature is *didactic*, aiming to teach a lesson/moral
- Early examples: *Aesop's Fables*, hornbooks, chapbooks, *Tales of Mother Goose*
- Materials were evolving over time, with paper books being uncommon at this time
- [[John Newberry]] begins publishing children's books

**1800's**:
- School is becoming more common for both genders
	- Bit of a class divide, as it was still prohibitively expensive for many
- Beginning of more non-didactic literature
	- *A Book of Nonsense*, *Alice in Wonderland*
- Beginning of "Golden Age" of children's literature

**1900's**:
- Big emphasis for imaginary worlds
	- Childhood represented as a magical/idyllic time
- *Peter Rabbit*, *The Wonderful Wizard of Oz*
- Caldecott Medal established (1937)
- Changes in Illustration between early 1900's and mid-1900's
	- Better technology allows for full color illustration

**mid-1900's**:
- Child labor laws increase in 1950's
- More children in school leads to bigger school libraries
	- More books!

**Modern Era**:
- Rise in multiculturalism in children's literature
	- All-time peak in the COVID-19 pandemic
- Increase in Reader Response Theory
	- Children should read for both information (efferent) and pleasure (aesthetic experience)
	- Meaning is the intersection of the text, reader interpretation, and the writer's intent
