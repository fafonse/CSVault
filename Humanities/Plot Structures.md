Different ways of the plot developing.

## Progressive
A linear plot structure that follows chronological order with a clear beginning, middle, and end.
> The plot structure of Percy Jackson


## Freytag's Pyramid
![400](https://writers.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/freytags_pyramid_diagram.jpg)

Focused more so on action than anything else, but tries to capture the intensity of the plot, not anything specific with characters.

## The Hero's Journey
A common structure of the "hero" and their character growth throughout the plot.

**Act 1** (in the *known* world):
1. Ordinary world
2. Call to adventure
3. Refusal to the call
4. Meet the mentor
5. Cross the first threshold
**Act 2** (in the *unknown* world):
6. Tests, allies, enemies
7. Innermost cave
8. The ordeal
9. Reward
**Act 3**: 
10. The road back
11. The resurrection
12. Return with elixir

## Circular
A cause-and-effect sequence of events that lead back to the first event.

Common in picture books and simpler stories for kids. Idea is that you end right where you started, or close enough to it.
> Children love to find cause-and-effect sequences (like buttons that make loud sounds)
## Episodic
Where each entry in the story is a small "episode" in a larger setting/structure. Episodic stories are by nature chunked out into tiny bits, and typically don't have to follow a singular timeline/progression.
Think *The Simpsons*, *Frog and Toad*, etc.

## Cumulative
All the previous events are repeated when a new event occurs. Typically seen in songs/rhymey stories. Really easy for preschoolers and younger readers to understand. 

> EX: *The Napping House*, *The Old Lady who Swallowed a Fly*, *12 Days of Christmas*

## Parallel
Plots in which each main character has a separate but related story line that merges in the end. These story lines occur simultaneously, and are somewhat related and generally merge into a larger narrative.

> EX: *Wonder* series, *The Stormlight Archive*

## Subplot
A secondary plot that supports the main plot of the story. Subplots can involve characters other than the protagonist or antagonist.

Subplots do not need to be related to the main plot much, and can feature wildly different settings and characters. They're essentially just a smaller story within the big picture. 
> EX: *Holes*, *Bones*
